The Emotional Qualities of Pie

Foreword

At first I thought, that was but a way to be reminded of home. After all, I had bought a recipe of pies from a Brooklyn bakery when I moved to Mexico. I thought I would make many pies. I thought through the pies I would not forget, and they would not forget me.

Then, one day, upon a sigh from the couch — this pasta is making me depressed, it said — a small seed was planted: what is the emotionality of food?

I remembered the surprise as I desended a mountain and discovered the plant underneath my foot: short blueberry bushes unnoticiable to an untrained eye. I inked my hands and lips red-purple and wondered if anyone could taste the contentment the same way I did.

If food could transmit feelings.

With such impetus, I made pies and noted down my fragments of emotions that led to the making of them (and later, other sweetlings). I picked ingredients from the mountains — not only because I was in a remote place during berry season, but what else would elicit the human emotions more than being in the unsheltered cold, streams hurrying into the sea?

These are about the emotionality in the creation. Some time in the future, I shall add my findings from the end of reception.

But for now, pies.

storm

  • Incessant rain.

  • Unprepared to face the piercing cold and my own clumsiness. Pants stuck to legs. Glasses fogged up. Wind sent the rain down my face. I was a bit angry in such discomfort.

  • Or was I in awe?

  • A thought frightened me. They might they never wake up again.

sun

  • Abruptly delared bed time at one a.m. and lay on the bed without falling sleeping. (Oh, the daze from aquavit.) The sun rose around four thirty.

  • Lethargic and warm after days of gloom.

  • Full from the reindeer stew at lunch, berries abundant at the height of my ankle.

Encounter with the clouds

  • Climbed a steep mountain by holding onto the, yes, moss.

    (What seems feeble can be incredibly strong. What's not to trust are the rocks. Parallel to their age is their readiness to break apart and descend.)

  • Exhausted yet calm after a full hour on the vertical. So thankful that I thought of bringing a container for harvesting cloudberries.

  • Between the berries' perference to grow near water and the untimely holes in my shoes, I was becoming accustomed to wet feet.

zzz. zzz.

  • Idle day.

    Quietness in the farm house.

    Cut rhubarb from the yard.

  • The wind almost blew the door away. Thank goodness.

    I was surely addicted to weather.

sweet home

  • Back from Karasjok today and went to the hill immediately. My body was tired on the mainland without the hikes.

  • Discovered blockberries, finally, after reading about them for so long.

  • Sat around the couch, surrounded by familiar laughters.

parting: the beginning of end

  • Three people left today: one in the morning, two in the evening.

  • Woke up at 5:30 to pick berries. Started baking at 7.

  • How would I be remembered?

    As a blueberry stain, perhaps?

departure

  • This morning I folded up the last pair of socks.

  • Outside the rhubarbs were getting old.

  • What was intended as picnic bars turned out to be a crumble.

  • "You are leaving, and your dessert is falling apart," someone joked.

  • Melancholy, like the wind, made my ears cold.

And that, shall be the end of it.
